The importance of the study of jewelleryfinds from Vesnave

In Vesnave, two mines were excavated by a team of German and Iranian archaeologists in several campaigns from 2001 to 2004 .2 They discovered, mainly in Cale Gar 1, a great number of artefacts, among them jewellery - including many beads, finger-rings, ear-rings and pendants - and fragments of metal sheets, as well as much else. It is remarkable that these objects were found in such a context at Vesnave, because in Iran and the wider the Near East, jewellery has been unearthed predominantlyin graves. To excavate such an amount of artefacts in the Cale Gar 1 has been, apart from their importance for the investigation of the pre-Islamic, religion a milestone for research into small finds in Iran and the the Near East in general.

The study of these objects is of great interest for the understanding of ritual practices at Vesnave, since establishing their chronology and provenance may help broaden knowledge of personal ornament in ancient Iran.

Gold-in-glass beads

As impressive as the etched beads are the so-called gold-in-glass beads, which have been discovered in Cale Gar 1. They have not been found in large numbers, but they are beautifully produced pieces for which a special technique was employed: a gold foil is applied on a glass core and then enclosed in translucent, colourless glass. This manufacturing technique gave them a fine appearance, and analysing it helps to date them. These beads were abundant from Antiquity to Medieval times, and have been discovered in Asia, as well as inEurope ,including Great Britain. On the island of Rhodes, remains of unfinished gold-in-glass beads were found and dated to the late 3rd cent. BC.

G. Boon and Spaer suggest Egypt as the region of origin for the gold-in-glass method of manufacture, while D. Barag argues that it emanated from Phoenician roots and other scholars prefer India. As an early instance of gold-in-glass manufacturing, the so-called Canosa Groupof the 1st half of the 3rd cent. BC has been considered by severalscholars. This refers to two well preserved glass bowls with decorated golden inlays found alongside eightglasses, in a grave in southern Italy, having probably been produced in a Ptolemaic workshop. According to D. Harden and A. Oliver, no gold-in-glass beadshave been found of an earlier date than the so-called Canosa Group Group. This fact, together with the remains of the glass workshop at Rhodes that date to the late 3rd century BC, implies that gold-in-glass beadswere not produced before the 3rd cent. BC, in particular the late 3rd century. As mentioned above, such beads continued to be produced into Medieval times, though evidence for continuity of manufacture in Islamic the Near East is scarce.

The gold-in-glass technique

Special skill and experience were needed to produce gold-in-glass beads. The craftsman had to put a flimsy gold sheet on a glass core and cover this combination with translucent glass. The two glasslayers should then respond equally to heat, i.e. they should have the same coefficient of expansion ; otherwise the glass would break during the process. Another challenge was not to harm the gold foil that could be as thin as 0.0001 mm, and so was very easily damaged.35 According to Spaer, earlier beads were finished individually, and show larger string-holes and a thicker exterior glass layer; these were of better quality. In later times beads were treated with tools and probably moulds.36 Grooves in the glass point to fabrication of the beads by drawing glass tubes “A drawn tubes was, after cooling, covered with a very thin layer of metal foil , probably attached withthe aid of an adhesive. Another, slightly larger, pre-manufactured tubes was then slipped over the first.”. This gold-in-glass tubes was then divided by a tool into single beads or into beads with two or more segments. Spaer writes furthermore: “(...) There is nonetheless a clear indication that the beads finished individually by hot-working are Hellenistic and probably, at least in part, also early Roman. Beads finished by the use of segmentingmolds are mainly Roman or later ”38

Conclusion

Of the many finds of Vesnave, just a few have been presented here, but they give an indication of the chronology of the jewellery deposited and found in mine 1. Although the sacrificial sites had already been in use from about the 8th cent. BC, obviously jewellery was deposited at Vesnave not earlier than 300 BC, centuries after the first ritual use of the caves as recorded in the votive deposit.

This agrees with beads and rings being distributed predominantly in the front part of cave 1. Additionally, artefacts show that there was a peak period for depositing jewellery in the centuries AD, a hypothesis supported by finds of coinsfrom the time of the Parthian and Sasanian kings . Furthermore, the Umayyad coinand discoveries of a finger-ring and beads indicate that people deposited objects at Vesnave until early Islamic times, albeit few.

The artefacts from Vesnave found in Cale Gar 1, Cale Gar 2are related to one another and seem to have a special meaning, since they were used as

votive pieces. Further research is needed better to understand how worship was practiced at Vesnave .

Nonetheless, based on the finds which are discussed in this article, some statements can be made already; jewellery always had a particular part to play in oblation, and in Cale Gar 1 a significant amount was found. Some categories of jewellery , such as the plain carnelian beads , seem to have been quite common, while others were more distinctive and hence valuable. For example, etched carnelian beads etched carnelian beads etched carnelian beads and gold-in-glass beads were especially precious objectsin a rural region in the ancient world,their manufacture requiring special skills. Like the amber beads discovered, they may be imports from far afield.60 As for the “bird” finger-rings, “bird” finger-rings. , their symbols andmeaning must have been fimiliar to the ancient people of Vesnave since they were deposited frequently. Summary

Jewellery incorporates a significant category among the finds from the caves Cale Gar 1, Cale Gar 2 at Vesnave. Selected find groups are described and compared to analogies from sites in Iran and elsewhere in the Near East. Most comparisons from Iran originate from the northern province of Gilan or the region of Germi in East Azarbaijan, whereas few are from Neisapur and Qasr-e Abu Nasr, respectively in northeastern and southern Iran. Beyond Iran, comparable finds come from Crimea, Egypt and Ed-Dur in Arabia. Already - as described here - etched carnelian beads, etched carnelian beads, etched carnelian beads, so-called gold-in-glass beads and severl finger-rings give an indication of the chronology of Cale Gar 1 jewellery deposits, and a preliminary assessment of their meaning can be made. Metal sheets and an arrow head are also discussed.
